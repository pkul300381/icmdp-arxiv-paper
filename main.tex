\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a rigorous mathematical framework for modeling autonomous
decision-making agents operating in stochastic and partially observable
environments, with a specific focus on applications in \emph{Agentic
Commerce}---the autonomous or semi-autonomous execution of commercial
transactions on behalf of human users. Classical Markov Decision Processes
(MDPs) provide a foundational structure for sequential decision-making under
uncertainty, while their extensions---Partially Observable MDPs (POMDPs) and
Constrained MDPs (CMDPs)---address limitations related to hidden state
information and operational constraints, respectively. However, these models
remain insufficient for capturing explicit human \emph{intent}, which is
inherently qualitative, state-dependent, and difficult to encode within
traditional reward or cost functions.

To address this gap, we introduce the \emph{Intent-Constrained Markov Decision
Process} (ICMDP), a novel extension that incorporates intent as a formal hard
constraint on the admissible action space. Unlike reward-based alignment
approaches such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), which
influence behavior indirectly through reward shaping, the ICMDP framework
enforces strict admissibility conditions that guarantee policy compliance with
user-defined intent at every decision step. The framework further integrates
Large Language Models (LLMs) as probabilistic intent parsers, translating
natural language instructions into structured constraint functions that govern
agent behavior.

Through theoretical analysis, we show that ICMDP and CMDP are expressively
incomparable: ICMDP enforces zero-violation per-step admissibility that no
CMDP can replicate, while CMDP captures trajectory-level budget feasibility
beyond any state-local filter. A worked example in Agentic Commerce
illustrates the framework under partial observability and dynamic intent, and
we analyze failure modes, RLHF comparisons, and open computational challenges.
\end{abstract}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Intent-Constrained MDP (ICMDP), Markov Decision
Processes, Constrained MDPs, Partially Observable MDPs, Intent Constraints,
Agentic Commerce, Large Language Models, AI Alignment, Safe Reinforcement
Learning.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Autonomous decision agents are increasingly deployed across robotics, automated
finance, logistics optimization, and digital commerce platforms. These agents
interact with environments through sequential cycles of observation, action
selection, and state transition. Because these interactions unfold over time
under uncertainty, rigorous mathematical frameworks are necessary to analyze and
optimize such systems.

Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) \citep{puterman1994} provide the classical
mathematical model for sequential decision-making under uncertainty. In an MDP,
an agent observes the current state of the system, selects an action according
to a policy, and receives a reward while the environment transitions to a new
state. The agent's objective is to find a policy that maximizes cumulative
discounted reward over time.

Real-world deployments, however, introduce three challenges that the canonical
MDP formalism does not adequately address:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=(\roman*)]
  \item \textbf{Partial observability.} Agents rarely have direct access to the
  true environmental state; instead, they observe noisy projections of it.
  \item \textbf{Operational constraints.} Budget caps, regulatory requirements,
  and safety limits impose hard feasibility conditions on admissible plans.
  \item \textbf{User intent.} Modern conversational interfaces allow users to
  specify goals in natural language. These specifications are qualitative and
  context-sensitive, and cannot be losslessly encoded as scalar reward signals.
\end{enumerate}

The rise of Large Language Models (LLMs) as the interface layer between humans
and agentic systems makes the third challenge particularly pressing. An agent
executing autonomous purchases on behalf of a user (an instance of
\emph{Agentic Commerce}) must interpret intent such as \emph{"buy me the
cheapest carbon-neutral option within my $\$100$ budget"} and translate it into
a formal decision policy---all while operating under stochastic market
conditions.

This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=(\arabic*)]
  \item We provide a self-contained review of MDPs, POMDPs, and CMDPs, locating
  each framework's expressiveness gap relative to explicit intent modeling
  (Section~\ref{sec:framework}).
  \item We formally define the \emph{Intent-Constrained Markov Decision Process}
  (ICMDP), which embeds intent as a state-dependent hard constraint on the
  admissible action space, and derive its associated Bellman optimality
  equations (Section~\ref{sec:icmdp}).
  \item We characterize the LLM as a probabilistic intent parser within this
  framework, establishing a formal separation of concerns between semantic
  interpretation and stochastic optimization
  (Section~\ref{sec:llm}).
  \item We work through a concrete Agentic Commerce scenario, map each
  architectural layer to a formal component of the ICMDP, and analyze
  theoretical properties, failure modes, and comparisons to RLHF
  (Sections~\ref{sec:example}--\ref{sec:discussion}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Markov Decision Processes.}
The theoretical foundations of MDPs and stochastic dynamic programming are
due to \citet{puterman1994}. The connection to modern reinforcement learning
(RL) algorithms is established in \citet{sutton2018}.

\paragraph{Partial observability.}
POMDPs were introduced to model environments in which the true system state
cannot be directly observed. \citet{kaelbling1998} developed belief-state
planning methods that maintain probability distributions over possible states
and showed tractable approximation algorithms for finite-horizon problems.
Recent work by \citet{levyehudi2023} addresses the computational challenge of
deploying POMDP-based systems in real time by proposing probabilistic
approximations of observation models with formal degradation guarantees.

\paragraph{Constrained reinforcement learning.}
CMDPs extend MDPs by constraining expected cumulative costs
\citep{altman1999}. \citet{achiam2017cpo} proposed Constrained Policy
Optimization (CPO), a practical algorithm for CMDPs that guarantees monotone
constraint satisfaction improvement. \citet{garcia2015} survey the broader
landscape of safe RL techniques.

\paragraph{Alignment and RLHF.}
The challenge of aligning increasingly capable systems with human preferences
is surveyed in \citet{leike2018}. \citet{christiano2017} introduced learning
from human feedback as a mechanism to approximate qualitative preferences,
forming the conceptual foundation for modern RLHF pipelines.

\paragraph{LLMs for planning.}
\citet{yao2023tot} proposed Tree of Thoughts (ToT), enabling LLMs to
deliberate over multiple reasoning paths before committing to a decision,
demonstrating that sequence models can support structured multi-step planning.
Foundational work on the attention mechanism underlying modern LLMs is due to
\citet{vaswani2017}.

\paragraph{Positioning of this work.}
To our knowledge, this work is among the first to formalize intent as a
state-dependent hard constraint on the admissible action space within an
MDP---which we term an \emph{Intent-Constrained MDP} (ICMDP). The
distinguishing features are: a state-dependent filter
$\cI(s) \subseteq \cA$ that enforces per-step admissibility
rather than expectation-based penalties; an LLM acting as a probabilistic
intent parser that grounds natural-language instructions in a formal
constraint structure; and zero violation probability by construction, which
expectation-based formulations cannot guarantee.

\section{Mathematical Framework}
\label{sec:framework}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Markov Decision Processes}
\label{subsec:mdp}

\begin{definition}[MDP]
A \emph{Markov Decision Process} is a tuple
$\cM = (\cS, \cA, \cP, R, \gamma)$, where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\cS$ is a finite set of states,
  \item $\cA$ is a finite set of actions,
  \item $\cP : \cS \times \cA \times \cS \to [0,1]$ is the transition
  probability function, with $\cP(s' \given s, a)$ denoting the probability
  of transitioning to state $s'$ upon taking action $a$ in state $s$,
  \item $R : \cS \times \cA \to \R$ is the reward function, and
  \item $\gamma \in [0,1)$ is the discount factor.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

A \emph{stationary policy} $\pi : \cS \to \Delta(\cA)$ specifies the
probability $\pi(a \given s)$ of selecting action $a$ in state $s$, where
$\Delta(\cA)$ denotes the probability simplex over $\cA$. The
\emph{state-value function} under policy $\pi$ is
\begin{equation}
  V^\pi(s) = \E_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t R(s_t, a_t)
  \,\Big|\, s_0 = s\right],
  \label{eq:value}
\end{equation}
and the \emph{optimal value function} satisfies the \emph{Bellman optimality
equation}:
\begin{equation}
  V^*(s) = \max_{a \in \cA}\!\left[R(s,a) + \gamma
    \sum_{s' \in \cS} \cP(s' \given s, a)\, V^*(s')\right].
  \label{eq:bellman}
\end{equation}
The corresponding optimal policy is
\begin{equation}
  \pi^*(s) = \argmax_{a \in \cA}\!\left[R(s,a) + \gamma
    \sum_{s' \in \cS} \cP(s' \given s, a)\, V^*(s')\right].
  \label{eq:optpol}
\end{equation}

The \emph{action-value function} (Q-function) $Q^\pi : \cS \times \cA \to \R$
provides a per-action decomposition of the value function:
\begin{equation}
  Q^\pi(s,a) = R(s,a) + \gamma \sum_{s' \in \cS}
    \cP(s' \given s, a)\, V^\pi(s').
  \label{eq:qfunc}
\end{equation}
The optimal Q-function satisfies the \emph{Q-learning Bellman equation}:
\begin{equation}
  Q^*(s,a) = R(s,a) + \gamma \sum_{s' \in \cS}
    \cP(s' \given s, a)\max_{a' \in \cA} Q^*(s', a'),
  \label{eq:qbellman}
\end{equation}
with $V^*(s) = \max_{a \in \cA} Q^*(s,a)$ and
$\pi^*(s) = \argmax_{a \in \cA} Q^*(s,a)$.
Equation~\eqref{eq:qbellman} is the basis for model-free Q-learning
algorithms \citep{sutton2018}, which estimate $Q^*$ from sampled transitions
without requiring explicit knowledge of $\cP$.

While elegant, the MDP formulation assumes full state observability, imposes
no operational constraints, and encodes the entire objective within the scalar
reward $R$. These assumptions are violated in Agentic Commerce, where (i)
prices and inventory levels are hidden, (ii) budgets and compliance rules must
be hard-respected, and (iii) qualitative user preferences cannot be reduced to
a single reward signal.

\subsection{Partially Observable MDPs}
\label{subsec:pomdp}

\begin{definition}[POMDP]
A \emph{Partially Observable Markov Decision Process} extends an MDP with an
observation model, forming the tuple
$\cM = (\cS, \cA, \mathcal{O}, \cP, \cZ, R, \gamma)$, where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\mathcal{O}$ is a finite set of observations, and
  \item $\cZ : \cS \times \cA \times \mathcal{O} \to [0,1]$ is the observation
  probability function, with $\cZ(o \given s', a)$ denoting the probability of
  observing $o$ after transitioning to $s'$ via action $a$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Because the true state is unobservable, the agent maintains a \emph{belief
state} $b \in \Delta(\cS)$, where $b(s)$ is the agent's probability estimate
that the current state is $s$. After taking action $a$ and observing $o$, the
belief is updated via Bayes' rule:
\begin{equation}
  b'(s') \propto \cZ(o \given s', a)\sum_{s \in \cS} \cP(s' \given s, a)\, b(s).
  \label{eq:belief_update}
\end{equation}
The value function is then defined over the belief simplex:
\begin{equation}
  V(b) = \max_{a \in \cA}\!\left[\sum_{s \in \cS} b(s)\, R(s,a)
    + \gamma \sum_{o \in \mathcal{O}} \Prob(o \given b, a)\,
    V\!\left(\tau(b, a, o)\right)\right],
  \label{eq:pomdp_value}
\end{equation}
where $\tau(b, a, o)$ is the updated belief after action $a$ yields
observation $o$, and $\Prob(o \given b, a) = \sum_{s,s'} \cZ(o \given s', a)
\cP(s' \given s, a) b(s)$.

\paragraph{POMDP in Agentic Commerce.}
In an agentic procurement setting, the hidden state $\cS$ encodes the true
minimum price a vendor will accept, actual inventory levels, and the user's
implicit urgency or budget. The agent receives observations $\mathcal{O}$ such
as public price listings, historical bid patterns, and stock-out signals. The
belief state $b$ is therefore the agent's probabilistic model of the market:
for example, \emph{"there is a 60\% probability this vendor will drop its
price by 5\% within two hours, but a 40\% risk the item sells out before
then."}

While the POMDP provides a principled model of uncertainty, solving it exactly
is PSPACE-hard \citep{kaelbling1998}. The computational burden is exacerbated
in real-time commerce, where negotiation must occur within milliseconds. This
motivates constraining the policy to a tractable feasible region via CMDP.

\subsection{Constrained MDPs}
\label{subsec:cmdp}

\begin{definition}[CMDP \citep{altman1999}]
A \emph{Constrained Markov Decision Process} augments an MDP with a set of
cost functions and associated thresholds:
$\cM_C = (\cS, \cA, \cP, R, \{C_k\}_{k=1}^n, \{d_k\}_{k=1}^n, \gamma)$,
where $C_k : \cS \times \cA \to \R$ is the $k$-th cost function and
$d_k \in \R$ is its threshold.
\end{definition}

The CMDP optimization problem is:
\begin{equation}
  \max_{\pi}\; \E_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t R(s_t, a_t)\right]
  \quad \text{subject to} \quad
  \E_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t C_k(s_t, a_t)\right] \leq d_k,
  \quad k = 1,\ldots,n.
  \label{eq:cmdp}
\end{equation}

A standard approach linearizes~\eqref{eq:cmdp} via Lagrange multipliers
$\bm{\lambda} = (\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n) \geq \mathbf{0}$
\citep{altman1999,achiam2017cpo}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\pi, \bm\lambda) =
  \E_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t R(s_t, a_t)\right]
  - \sum_{k=1}^{n} \lambda_k
    \!\left(\E_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t C_k(s_t,a_t)\right]
    - d_k\right).
  \label{eq:lagrangian}
\end{equation}
The optimal policy is obtained by solving the saddle-point problem
$\min_{\bm\lambda \geq 0} \max_\pi \mathcal{L}(\pi, \bm\lambda)$.

\paragraph{CMDP in Agentic Commerce.}
Typical constraint dimensions include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Budget}: $\E_\pi[\text{total spend}] \leq \$100$.
  \item \textbf{Vendor trust}: $\E_\pi[\text{fraction of untrusted vendors}]
  \leq 2\%$.
  \item \textbf{Latency}: expected transaction completion time $\leq 40\,
  \text{seconds}$.
  \item \textbf{Regulatory compliance}: ISO 20022 header validation must
  succeed with probability $\geq 0.99$.
\end{itemize}
Combining partial observability with cost constraints yields a framework for
\emph{verifiable intent} (VI): the belief state handles probabilistic
uncertainty while constraints provide hard operational boundaries.

A critical limitation of CMDP, however, is that constraints are enforced only
\emph{in expectation}. The agent may occasionally violate a constraint provided
the long-run average remains below the threshold. This is insufficient for
qualitative intent specifications such as \emph{"never transact with an
unverified seller"}, which admit no violations at all.

\section{The Intent-Constrained MDP (ICMDP)}
\label{sec:icmdp}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Formal Definition}

\begin{definition}[ICMDP]
\label{def:icmdp}
An \emph{Intent-Constrained Markov Decision Process} is a tuple
\[
  \cM_I = (\cS,\, \cA,\, \cP,\, R,\, \gamma,\, \cI),
\]
where $(\cS, \cA, \cP, R, \gamma)$ are defined as in a standard MDP and
$\cI : \cS \to 2^{\cA}$ is the \emph{intent-constraint function} that maps
each state to the set of \emph{intent-admissible} actions.
\end{definition}

The ICMDP imposes a \emph{hard admissibility condition} on every policy:
\begin{equation}
  \pi(a \given s) = 0 \quad \forall\, a \notin \cI(s),\; \forall\, s \in \cS.
  \label{eq:hard_constraint}
\end{equation}

This is strictly stronger than the expectation-based constraints of
CMDP~\eqref{eq:cmdp}, which allow occasional violations. The
\emph{intent-feasible action set} at state $s$ is
\begin{equation}
  \cA_\cI(s) \coloneqq \{ a \in \cA : a \in \cI(s) \}.
  \label{eq:feasible_set}
\end{equation}
The \emph{admissible policy class} is
\begin{equation}
  \Pi_\cI \coloneqq \bigl\{ \pi \in \Delta(\cA)^\cS :
    \pi(a \given s) = 0 \;\;\forall\, a \notin \cI(s),\;
    \forall\, s \in \cS \bigr\}.
  \label{eq:policy_class}
\end{equation}

\subsection{ICMDP Bellman Equations}
\label{subsec:icmdp_bellman}

The optimal ICMDP value function satisfies the \emph{intent-constrained Bellman
optimality equation}:
\begin{equation}
  V_\cI^*(s) = \max_{a \in \cA_\cI(s)}\!\left[R(s,a) + \gamma
    \sum_{s' \in \cS} \cP(s' \given s, a)\, V_\cI^*(s')\right],
  \label{eq:icmdp_bellman}
\end{equation}
with the corresponding optimal intent-constrained policy
\begin{equation}
  \pi_\cI^*(s) = \argmax_{a \in \cA_\cI(s)}\!\left[R(s,a) + \gamma
    \sum_{s' \in \cS} \cP(s' \given s, a)\, V_\cI^*(s')\right].
  \label{eq:icmdp_optpol}
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}[Reduction to MDP]
When $\cI(s) = \cA$ for all $s$, the ICMDP reduces to a standard MDP.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Feasibility and Infeasibility}
\label{subsec:feasibility}

A fundamental precondition for meaningful policy optimization under ICMDP is
\emph{intent feasibility}: there must exist at least one admissible action in
every reachable state.

\begin{definition}[Intent Feasibility]
\label{def:feasibility}
An ICMDP $\cM_I$ is \emph{intent-feasible} if and only if
\begin{equation}
  \cA_\cI(s) \neq \emptyset \quad \forall\, s \in \cS.
  \label{eq:feasibility}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

If feasibility fails at some state $s^*$, then $\cA_\cI(s^*) = \emptyset$
and the Bellman recursion~\eqref{eq:icmdp_bellman} is undefined for that
state. In this case, the system must either (a) relax the constraint
$\cI(s^*)$ (\emph{constraint softening}), or (b) surface a
\emph{Constraint Violation Warning} to the user, requesting intent
re-specification. This introduces a \emph{meta-decision layer} that operates
above the ICMDP itself---a novel architectural requirement absent from
classical CMDPs.

\subsection{Relationship to CMDP: Expressiveness}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:strict_generalization}
ICMDP and CMDP are \emph{expressively incomparable}: there exist
intent-admissible policy classes $\Pi_\cI$ that cannot be represented as the
feasible set of any CMDP, and there exist CMDP-feasible policy sets that
cannot be represented as any ICMDP admissible policy class.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
(\emph{ICMDP $\not\subseteq$ CMDP}.)
Consider the intent \emph{"never select action $a^-$ in state $s^+$"},
encoded as $\cI(s^+) = \cA \setminus \{a^-\}$, so $\Pi_\cI$ contains only
policies with $\pi(a^- \given s^+) = 0$. No CMDP with finite multipliers
$\lambda_k$ can enforce this via~\eqref{eq:lagrangian}: because CMDP
constraints are expectation-based, the Lagrangian assigns finite penalty to
selecting $a^-$ once, so the CMDP-optimal policy may place nonzero probability
on $a^-$ in $s^+$.

(\emph{CMDP $\not\subseteq$ ICMDP}.)
Consider a CMDP constraint $\E_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t
C(s_t, a_t)\right] \leq d$ for a cost function $C > 0$ everywhere.
The CMDP-feasible set is determined by the entire trajectory distribution:
whether action $a$ is permissible in state $s$ depends on the cumulative costs
incurred in all other states. No state-dependent filter
$\cI : \cS \to 2^{\cA}$ can capture this trajectory-level dependency, because
$\cI(s)$ is fixed regardless of the history of costs accumulated along the
path to $s$. Hence the CMDP-feasible policy set cannot in general be expressed
as any $\Pi_\cI$. $\square$
\end{proof}

\subsection{Dynamic Intent}
\label{subsec:dynamic}

Classical MDP formulations assume that the reward and transition functions are
stationary. In long-running Agentic Commerce workflows, user intent may change
mid-execution:
\begin{equation}
  \cI_t(s) \neq \cI_{t'}(s) \quad \text{for some } t \neq t'.
  \label{eq:dynamic_intent}
\end{equation}
This introduces a \emph{non-stationary} constrained decision process. The
ICMDP framework accommodates two responses:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Constraint injection}: Real-time update of $\cI_t(s)$ without
  full model retraining.
  \item \textbf{Feasibility alerts}: Proactive notification when market
  conditions render current intent constraints infeasible
  (Definition~\ref{def:feasibility}).
\end{enumerate}
Efficient online algorithms for dynamically reconfigured ICMDPs remain an open
research problem. The next section presents solution methods for ICMDP.

\subsection{Solutions for ICMDP}
\label{subsec:solutions}
The ICMDP formulation modifies the feasible action space rather than the reward 
or cost structure. As a result, standard dynamic programming and reinforcement 
learning algorithms can be adapted to operate over the intent-restricted action set
$\cA_\cI(s)$. This yields a class of solution methods that we refer to as 
intent-filtered optimization, in which infeasible actions are 
pruned prior to policy evaluation and improvement.

\subsubsection{Intent-Constrained Value Iteration}
The Bellman optimality update for ICMDP~\eqref{eq:icmdp_bellman} can be implemented via a straightforward
modification of classical value iteration:

\begin{equation}
  V_{k+1}(s) = \max_{a \in \cA_\cI(s)}\left[R(s,a) + \gamma 
  \sum_{s' \in \cS} \cP(s' \given s, a)\, V_k(s')\right].
  \label{eq:intent_vk}
\end{equation}

The only modification relative to standard MDP value iteration is the restriction
of the maximization operator to the admissible set $\cA_\cI(s)$. This is equivalent
to state-dependent action masking applied prior to the Bellman backup
\citep{huang2022}.

Additionally, the per-iteration computation complexity reduces from $O(|\cS||\cA|)$ to 
$O(|\cS||\cA_\cI(s)|)$, which can generate efficiency gains when the intent 
constraints are sufficiently restrictive.

\subsubsection{Policy Iteration under Admissibility Constraints}

Policy iteration can be adapted by constraining both policy evaluation and improvement steps.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Policy Evaluation: The evaluation does not change, but it is applied to an admissible policy
    $\pi \in \Pi_\cI$.

    \item Policy Improvement:
           \begin{equation}
             \pi_{k+1}(s) = \argmax_{a \in \cA_\cI(s)} Q^{\pi_k}(s,a)
             \quad \forall\, s \in \cS.
           \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

Unlike CMDPs, no Lagrangian relaxation is required; constraint satisfaction is
guaranteed by construction of the policy class $\Pi_\cI$.

\subsubsection{Model-Free RL with Action Masking}
In model-free settings, ICMDP can be implemented via action masking in Q-learning
and policy gradient algorithms \citep{huang2022}.

Let $\mathcal{M}(s,a)$ denote the action masking function, where $\mathcal{M}(s,a) = 1$ 
if $a \in \cA_\cI(s)$ and $\mathcal{M}(s,a) = 0$ otherwise.

Then the Q-learning update rule can be modified as
\begin{equation}
  Q(s,a) \leftarrow Q(s,a) + \alpha\!\left[R(s,a)
    + \gamma \max_{a' \in \cA_\cI(s')} Q(s',a') - Q(s,a)\right],
  \quad a \in \cA_\cI(s).
\end{equation}

For policy gradient, the policy can be parameterized as

\begin{equation}
  \pi_\theta(a \given s) = \frac{\exp(Q(s,a))}{\sum_{a' \in \cA_\cI(s)} \exp(Q(s,a'))}
\end{equation}

where $Q(s,a)$ is the action-value function of a Deep Q-Network (DQN)
\citep{mnih2015}.

This ensures zero probability mass is assigned to inadmissible actions at all times, 
enforcing hard constraint satisfaction during both training and execution. 

\subsubsection{Feasibility Aware Planning}

A critical requirement for all solution methods is intent feasibility (Definition~\ref{def:feasibility}).
In practice, algorithms must incorporate feasibility checks:

\begin{itemize}
    \item If $\cA_\cI(s) = \emptyset$, the system must trigger constraint relaxation or a fallback policy.
    \item Maintain a feasibility monitor during planning and execution.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Comparison to CMDP solvers}

Unlike CMDP solution methods, which rely on Lagrangian dual optimization and may require 
iterative tuning of multipliers, ICMDP eliminates constraint violations by restricting 
the policy space a priori. This removes the need for saddle-point optimization but 
introduces sensitivity to constraint specification and feasibility.

\section{LLM as Probabilistic Intent Parser}
\label{sec:llm}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

Let $l \in \cL$ denote a natural language instruction provided by the user,
where $\cL$ is the space of all possible linguistic inputs.

\begin{definition}[Intent Parser]
An \emph{LLM-based intent parser} is a parametric mapping
\begin{equation}
  f_\theta : \cL \times \cS \to 2^{\cA},
  \qquad (l, s) \mapsto \cI(s),
  \label{eq:llm_map}
\end{equation}
where $\theta$ denotes the model parameters and the output $\cI(s)$ defines
the intent-admissible action set for state $s$ given the instruction $l$.
\end{definition}

Unlike traditional parsing systems that rely on predefined grammars, LLMs
perform this mapping probabilistically using contextual embeddings and
attention mechanisms \citep{vaswani2017}. The role of $f_\theta$ within the
ICMDP pipeline is not to optimize actions or policies directly, but to generate
a constraint set governing admissible decisions.

This formulation introduces a clean \emph{separation of concerns}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $f_\theta$ defines the constraint space $\cI(s)$ via semantic
  interpretation of $l$.
  \item The ICMDP optimization~\eqref{eq:icmdp_bellman} operates over that
  constrained space via stochastic dynamic programming.
\end{itemize}
As a result, improvements in language understanding (better $f_\theta$) do
not require changes to the underlying decision algorithms, and vice versa.

\paragraph{Comparison with RLHF.}
In RLHF \citep{christiano2017,leike2018}, human preferences are encoded in a
learned reward model $\hat{R}_\phi(s, a)$, and the agent maximizes
$\E_\pi[\hat{R}_\phi]$. This is an inherently \emph{soft} mechanism: undesired
actions are discouraged but not prohibited. In contrast, the ICMDP intent
parser produces \emph{hard} exclusions: $\pi(a \given s) = 0$ for all
$a \notin \cI(s)$, regardless of the reward assigned to that action. The two
approaches are complementary rather than competing: RLHF can be used to
refine behavior \emph{within} the feasible region defined by ICMDP, yielding a
hybrid where ICMDP ensures safety and intent compliance and RLHF ensures
qualitative optimization within those safe bounds.

\paragraph{Error propagation.}
The mapping $f_\theta$ is probabilistic and subject to misinterpretation. Since
constraint errors in ICMDP produce hard exclusions, the downstream effects can
be severe: valid actions may be entirely eliminated (\emph{constraint
over-tightening}) or prohibited actions may remain admissible (\emph{constraint
under-specification}). This highlights the importance of verification
mechanisms---e.g., formal consistency checking of the extracted $\cI(s)$
against a formal ontology---as a necessary preprocessing step before policy
optimization.

\section{Worked Example: Agentic Commerce}
\label{sec:example}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

We illustrate the ICMDP framework with a concrete Agentic Commerce scenario: a
user instructs an AI agent, via a conversational interface, to \emph{"Purchase
100 units of Product X from a carbon-neutral supplier, spending no more than
\$500, and completing the transaction within 60 seconds."} The agent must
autonomously search product catalogs, evaluate suppliers, negotiate terms, and
execute payment, all without further user intervention.

\subsection{Architectural Layers}

At a structural level, the Agentic Commerce pipeline decomposes into four
distinct layers, each corresponding to a formal component of the ICMDP
framework (Table~\ref{tab:layers}).

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering
  \caption{Agentic Commerce architectural layers and their formal ICMDP
  correspondences.}
  \label{tab:layers}
  \begin{tabular}{>{\bfseries}p{2.8cm} p{3.2cm} p{6.5cm}}
    \toprule
    Layer & Component & Formal Role \\
    \midrule
    User / Intent  & LLM Intent Parser ($f_\theta$) &
      Translates natural language $l$ into $\cI(s)$; initializes the
      admissible action space. \\[4pt]
    Perception      & POMDP Belief Updater &
      Maintains belief $b(s)$ over hidden market states (prices, inventory,
      seller reliability) via~\eqref{eq:belief_update}. \\[4pt]
    Decision        & ICMDP Solver &
      Solves~\eqref{eq:icmdp_bellman} over $\cA_\cI(s)$, producing the
      optimal intent-admissible policy $\pi_\cI^*$. \\[4pt]
    Settlement      & Payment Switch / Ledger &
      Executes the chosen action and logs a \emph{certificate of execution}:
      a mathematical trace proving each action was within $\cI(s)$ at every
      step. \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{State and Action Spaces}

The state space $\cS$ includes tuples of the form
$(p_{\text{ask}}, \text{inv}, \text{eco}, \text{rep})$, where $p_{\text{ask}}$
is the seller's posted ask price, $\text{inv} \in \{0,1\}$ indicates
availability, $\text{eco} \in \{0,1\}$ encodes carbon-neutrality
certification, and $\text{rep} \in [0,1]$ is the seller's verifiable
reputation score.

The action space $\cA$ includes: \textsc{Query}($v$), \textsc{Bid}($v, p$),
\textsc{Accept}($v$), \textsc{Reject}($v$), and \textsc{Pay}($v, \text{rail}$)
for vendor $v$ and price $p$.

\subsection{Intent Parsing}

The LLM parses the user's instruction into the following formal constraint
function:
\begin{align}
  \cI(s) = \bigl\{\, a \in \cA \;\big|\;&
    \text{eco}(v(a)) = 1 \;\wedge\;
    p(a) \leq \$500 \;\wedge\;
    t_{\text{elapsed}} \leq 60\,\text{s} \bigr\},
  \label{eq:example_intent}
\end{align}
where $v(a)$ is the vendor associated with action $a$ and $p(a)$ is the
transaction price. Any action involving a non-certified supplier, an
out-of-budget price, or a latency violation is \emph{hard-excluded} from the
policy.

\subsection{Belief State and POMDP Layer}

The true minimum price a vendor will accept is hidden. The agent maintains
belief $b_t$ over possible ask prices and updates it upon receiving
observations (e.g., a public listing or a counter-offer). For instance, at
time $t$:
\[
  b_t(\text{ask} = \$4.80/\text{unit}) = 0.60, \quad
  b_t(\text{ask} = \$5.10/\text{unit}) = 0.40.
\]
The agent decides whether to accept immediately or wait for a better quote
by evaluating the POMDP value function~\eqref{eq:pomdp_value} restricted to
$\cA_\cI(s)$.

\subsection{Verification Certificate}

Because the constraint $\cI(s)$ is embedded in the Bellman
recursion~\eqref{eq:icmdp_bellman}, the agent can produce a certificate of
execution for each time step $t$:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Observed belief}: $b_t$, representing current market assessment.
  \item \textbf{Admissible set}: $\cA_\cI(s_t)$, the intent-filtered action
  space.
  \item \textbf{Chosen action}: $a_t^* \in \cA_\cI(s_t)$, with proof that
  $a_t^* \in \cI(s_t)$.
\end{enumerate}
This trace provides the user with formal evidence that the agent operated
within intent boundaries throughout the transaction---a guarantee unavailable
in reward-shaped or expectation-constrained systems.

\section{Results and Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Theoretical Properties of ICMDP}

The ICMDP framework introduces two principal structural properties relative to
CMDP.

\paragraph{Hard pruning of the policy space.}
In a standard MDP or CMDP, the feasible policy class is
$\Pi = \{\pi : \cS \to \Delta(\cA)\}$ (with expected cost constraints). ICMDP
restricts this to $\Pi_\cI$ as defined in~\eqref{eq:policy_class}. This
pruning has two important consequences:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Strong safety and alignment.} Infeasible actions are eliminated
  prior to optimization rather than penalized post hoc, consistent with the
  principles of safe RL \citep{garcia2015}.
  \item \textbf{Reduced exploration complexity.} The agent need not evaluate
  actions outside the intent boundary. However, this benefit is conditional on
  the constraint not being overly restrictive (see feasibility
  discussion in Section~\ref{subsec:feasibility}).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Expressiveness gain over CMDP.}
CMDP enforces constraints in expectation:
\begin{equation}
  \E_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t C_k(s_t, a_t)\right] \leq d_k.
  \label{eq:cmdp_constraint}
\end{equation}
ICMDP enforces admissibility at every step:
\begin{equation}
  \pi(a \given s) = 0 \quad \forall\, a \notin \cI(s),\; \forall\, s.
  \label{eq:icmdp_constraint}
\end{equation}
Condition~\eqref{eq:icmdp_constraint} captures qualitative intent
specifications---such as \emph{"only interact with verified sellers"}
or \emph{"avoid environmentally harmful options"}---that cannot be enforced
by any CMDP with finite multipliers. Conversely, trajectory-level
budget constraints expressible in CMDP have no equivalent state-local
representation in ICMDP. Proposition~\ref{prop:strict_generalization}
formalizes this expressiveness incomparability.

\subsection{ICMDP vs.\ RLHF: Structural Distinctions}

Table~\ref{tab:comparison} summarizes the key structural distinctions between
RLHF, CMDP, and ICMDP.

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering
  \caption{Comparison of alignment mechanisms.}
  \label{tab:comparison}
  \begin{tabular}{lccc}
    \toprule
    Property & RLHF & CMDP & ICMDP \\
    \midrule
    Constraint type        & Soft (reward shaping) & Expectation & Hard (admissibility) \\
    Intent representation  & Implicit ($\hat{R}_\phi$) & Cost thresholds & $\cI(s)$ function \\
    Violation guarantee    & None (statistical) & In expectation & Zero (per step) \\
    Qualitative intent     & Approximate & Limited & Full \\
    Computational cost     & Training-time & Policy optimization & + LLM inference \\
    Verifiability          & Low & Moderate & High \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

RLHF and ICMDP address alignment from fundamentally different perspectives:
RLHF modifies the reward function \citep{christiano2017}, whereas ICMDP
constrains the action space. The two are complementary: RLHF can refine
behavior qualitatively \emph{within} the feasible region defined by ICMDP,
yielding a hybrid that separates capability optimization from constraint
enforcement \citep{achiam2017cpo}.

\subsection{Failure Modes and Open Problems}

\paragraph{LLM misinterpretation.}
The intent constraint function $\cI(s)$ is derived from the probabilistic
mapping $f_\theta$. Errors in natural language understanding can produce
incorrect constraint sets. Unlike reward errors in RLHF---which degrade
performance gradually---constraint errors in ICMDP can cause catastrophic
infeasibility, where valid actions are excluded entirely. Formal verification
of LLM-extracted constraints against consistent ontologies is a necessary
safeguard.

\paragraph{Dynamic intent non-stationarity.}
Classical MDP formulations assume stationary reward and transition functions.
Dynamic intent (Section~\ref{subsec:dynamic}) violates this assumption,
requiring either full re-optimization or incremental constraint updates.
Efficient online algorithms for dynamic ICMDPs are an open research area.

\paragraph{Empty feasible set.}
If $\cI$ is overly restrictive, $\cA_\cI(s) = \emptyset$ for some state
$s$, and~\eqref{eq:icmdp_bellman} is undefined. Resolution requires either
constraint relaxation or meta-level user interaction, introducing architectural
complexity beyond the base ICMDP.

\paragraph{Computational cost of LLM inference.}
The proposed framework invokes LLMs for initial intent parsing, intermediate
alignment checks, and policy validation. Given the latency and cost
characteristics of large-scale models, this creates a bottleneck for real-time
systems. Caching, intent pre-compilation, and approximation strategies can
mitigate this, but introduce trade-offs between accuracy, cost, and
responsiveness.

\subsection{Limitations}

The analysis in this paper is purely theoretical. No quantitative policy
performance comparison has been conducted; solver scalability has not been
empirically tested; and real-world robustness under noisy intent extraction
has not been evaluated. Empirical validation in practical Agentic Commerce
settings is necessary for a definitive conclusion.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------

We have established that while classical MDP, POMDP, and CMDP frameworks
provide progressively richer models for uncertainty and constraint handling,
they remain fundamentally limited in their ability to represent explicit,
state-dependent human intent. The proposed ICMDP framework addresses this gap
by introducing intent as a first-class mathematical constraint that directly
governs the admissible action space, shifting the optimization paradigm from
reward-driven maximization to intent-preserving decision processes.

Through formal analysis, we have shown that ICMDP and CMDP are expressively
incomparable (Proposition~\ref{prop:strict_generalization}): ICMDP admits
policies that no CMDP can enforce via expectation-based constraints, while
CMDP captures trajectory-level budget feasibility that no state-local intent
filter can replicate. The worked Agentic Commerce example
demonstrates architectural realizability and identifies the LLM-based intent
parser as a principled, modular component that separates semantic
interpretation from stochastic optimization.

Several important directions remain open:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Development of efficient solution algorithms for dynamically changing
  intent constraint sets, including online variants of value iteration and
  policy gradient methods.
  \item Formal verification mechanisms for LLM-derived intent mappings,
  ensuring consistency and alignment before policy optimization.
  \item Hybrid architectures integrating ICMDP with learning-based approaches
  such as RLHF, where RLHF refines qualitative behavior within the feasible
  region defined by ICMDP.
  \item Empirical validation across large-scale, real-time systems to evaluate
  computational feasibility, robustness under noisy intent extraction, and
  alignment fidelity.
\end{enumerate}

Overall, ICMDP offers a theoretically grounded direction for advancing
autonomous decision-making systems toward more reliable and human-aligned
behavior in increasingly complex and stochastic environments.
